The old bookstore on Maple Street had stood there for over eighty years, its faded brick facade worn smooth by countless rainstorms and summer heatwaves. Inside, the scent of aging paper and wood polish hung in the air like a gentle perfume, while shafts of afternoon sunlight filtered through the dusty windows, illuminating thousands of spines that stretched from floor to ceiling. Margaret had been coming here since she was twelve, when she first discovered a worn copy of "The Secret Garden" tucked away in a forgotten corner. Now, at seventy-three, she still visited every Tuesday, running her fingers along the shelves as if greeting old friends. The owner, a soft-spoken man named Arthur who had inherited the shop from his own father, always kept a pot of Earl Grey tea brewing near the register. Neither of them spoke much during these visits—they understood that some friendships thrived not on words, but on the comfortable silence shared between two people who simply enjoyed existing in the same space, surrounded by stories waiting to be told.
